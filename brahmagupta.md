### Explanation:
While this rule applies to general functions and not just Sines, we will treat it as it applies to the Sine function, specifically `sin(A + x)`. The rule translates as follows (p. 88):  
*(Notes in brackets are mine.)*

Consider the following Sine table:  
- The **‚Äútabular difference crossed over‚Äù** is `sin(A) - sin(A - 225')`, where `sin(A - 225')` is the tabulated value preceding `sin(A)`, meaning we have already passed, or ‚Äúcrossed over‚Äù this Sine difference on our table of Sines.  
- The **‚Äútabular difference to be crossed over‚Äù** refers to `sin(A + 225') - sin(A)`, where `sin(A + 225')` is the next tabulated Sine value after `sin(A)`, so we have not ‚Äúcrossed over‚Äù this Sine difference yet.  
- The **‚Äúresidual arc‚Äù** is `x`, where `0' < x < 225'`.  
- The **‚Äúcommon [tabulated] interval‚Äù** is the constant value by which our Sine table increases, `225'`.

> **‚ÄúMultiply half the difference of the tabular differences crossed over [`sin(A) - sin(A - 225')`] and to be crossed over [`sin(A + 225') - sin(A)`] by the residual arc [`x`] and divide by (the common [tabulated] interval [`225'`]). By the result (so obtained) increase or decrease half the sum of the same (two) differences, according as this [average] is less or greater than the difference to be crossed over. We get the true functional differences to be crossed over [the difference between `sin(A)` and `sin(A + x)`].‚Äù**
